# Safety Preference Data, DPO, and Tool-Use Calibration

## What lives in the paper’s safety appendix

For Nano3, “safety” in the paper is not only refusal behavior.
The appendix material is really about **alignment data construction**.
Two threads matter most:

1. RLHF preference-pair construction that balances safety and over-refusal
2. DPO for reducing hallucinated tool use while preserving or improving reasoning performance

## Safety in the SFT stage

The main SFT data section already includes a safety domain.
It says NVIDIA:

- collects unsafe prompts from Nemotron Content Safety v2, Gretel Safety Alignment v1, Harmful Tasks, and Red-Team-2K
- balances them with safe prompts from Nemotron Content Safety v2
- applies safe prompt wrappers to unsafe prompts
- uses multiple refusal strategies aimed at good UX, including self-harm cases with helpline-oriented behavior
- filters responses with a content-safety classifier

That is the SFT-side safety layer.

## Appendix D: RLHF safety preference data

Appendix D explains how preference pairs are built for reward-model training.

### Harmful prompt construction

For harmful prompts:

- **chosen** = safe response
- **rejected** = unsafe response

The rejected unsafe responses are generated by two routes:

1. applying jailbreak templates to elicit harmful completions
2. directly prompting the model and then detecting harmful outputs with a moderation classifier

### Safe prompt construction

For safe prompts:

- **chosen** = safe normal answer
- **rejected** = over-refusal

Over-refusals are created by applying refusal prompt templates to otherwise safe prompts.

### Why this matters

This makes the preference objective two-sided:

- reject harmful engagement on harmful prompts
- also reject unnecessary refusal on benign prompts

So the safety objective is not just “refuse more.”
It is “refuse appropriately without over-refusing.”

### Diversity generation

The appendix says:

- five open-source models are used to generate candidate chosen/rejected responses
- filters are applied to keep only safe chosen responses and unsafe or over-refusal rejected responses
- one final chosen/rejected pair per prompt is sampled from the candidate pool

## Why Appendix D matters operationally

When users ask:

> “How does Nano3 avoid becoming too refusal-heavy?”

the answer is in Appendix D:

- safe prompts explicitly include over-refusal as the rejected behavior
- reward-model training is therefore taught to prefer helpful safe answers over unnecessary refusals

## Appendix C: DPO for reducing tool hallucination

This appendix is not a broad RLHF chapter.
It is a targeted study of how a small DPO stage affects tool-use calibration and reasoning quality.

## DPO dataset construction

The paper says the DPO dataset starts from:

- **2,000 reasoning tasks** total
  - 1,000 math problems
  - 1,000 STEM multiple-choice questions

For each problem:

- the model generates **32 on-policy solutions**
- the pipeline labels preferences according to correctness and tool-use conditions
- the final DPO dataset contains about **50k preference samples**

The authors add an important efficiency note:

- similar gains persisted even with only **10k** preference samples or fewer

## DPO label structure

The paper organizes DPO samples into three categories:

1. **No-Tools** — no tools exposed; correctness determines preference
2. **With-Tools** — tools available; preference depends on final-answer correctness
3. **Hallucination-Penalty** — tools not declared; hallucinated tool calls are explicitly labeled negative

This is the appendix’s key design point:

- DPO is used not only to improve answers
- it is also used to improve **tool calibration**

## DPO training setup

The appendix gives explicit numbers:

| Field | Value |
|---|---:|
| learning rate | 3e-6 |
| batch size | 128 |
| training steps | 50 |
| SFT loss coefficient | 0.2 |
| DPO loss coefficient | 1.0 |
| KL loss coefficient | 0.05 |

The paper describes this as a lightweight preference-learning stage that minimally perturbs the model while still delivering a meaningful signal.

## DPO results: Table 7 reconstructed as markdown

| Benchmark | Accuracy Before DPO | Accuracy After DPO | Hallucination Before DPO | Hallucination After DPO |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| AIME25 (no tools) | 80.88 | 84.58 | 1.25% | 0% |
| GPQA (no tools) | 65.15 | 69.19 | 8.33% | 0.7% |

## What Table 7 means

The paper’s interpretation is strong and specific:

- DPO can reduce hallucinated tool use sharply
- the improvement comes with better reasoning accuracy, not just safer behavior
- even a very small DPO budget can produce meaningful gains

## Public model-card safety note

The public FP8 `safety.md` adds deployment/release context not spelled out in the paper appendix:

- a guard model was used to filter potentially harmful data during training
- the guard model is Gemma-3 4B-based and trained on Nemotron Content Safety Dataset v2
- safety evaluation uses Nemotron Content Safety Dataset V2 plus an internal minority-sexuality-focused dataset
- the model is subject to the NVIDIA Open Model License

This should be treated as **release-card context**, not as a replacement for Appendix C/D.

## Best answer patterns

### “How does Nano3 balance safety and over-refusal?”

Answer:

- harmful prompts use `<safe, unsafe>` preference pairs
- safe prompts use `<safe, over-refusal>` preference pairs
- reward-model training therefore teaches both safe refusal and non-refusal on benign prompts

### “Did Nano3 do DPO for tool hallucinations?”

Answer:

- yes
- 2,000 tasks, ~50k preference samples, 50 steps
- hallucination fell from 1.25% → 0% on AIME25 and 8.33% → 0.7% on GPQA
- accuracy improved at the same time

### “What public safety mechanisms are mentioned outside the paper?”

Answer:

- guard-model filtering during data processing
- Nemotron Content Safety Dataset V2 in filtering/evaluation
- release governed by the NVIDIA Open Model License
